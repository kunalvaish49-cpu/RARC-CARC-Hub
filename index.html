N846 SUPPLEMENTAL CO-16

NDC Does Not Correspond to HCPCS/CPT Billed

Drug Billing / Data Mismatch · Professional & Facility Claims ·
Common CARC Pairing
CMS-1500 Box 24 & UB-04 (FL43-FL44) CO-16 Claim/service lacks information
What This Means
💡
What This Means

The National Drug Code (NDC) reported on the claim does not match the HCPCS/CPT drug code (typically a J-code) billed on the same service line.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
N846 does not automatically mean the drug is not covered or the claim is permanently denied. It means the NDC and J-code need to match up before processing can continue.
Where to Report This Information
📄
CMS-1500 (Professional)
Professional claim reporting guidance
  • Box 24D HCPCS/CPT drug code (J-code)
  • Shaded area 24A–24G N4 qualifier + 11-digit NDC + unit of measure + quantity
✅ Correct: N412345678901
❌ Incorrect: N4 12345678901
🏥
UB-04 (Facility Claims)
Facility claim reporting guidance
  • FL - 44 HCPCS/CPT code
  • FL - 43 N4 + 11-digit NDC + UOM + Quantity
Format: N4 + 11-digit NDC + UOM + Qty
Example: ✅ Correct: N412345678901
⚠️
N4 Qualifier Format — Common Sub-Error
The N4 qualifier must appear as a single unspaced prefix directly before the 11-digit NDC. A space between N4 and the NDC number will cause a format rejection even if the NDC itself is correct.

💊

Key Rule
The UOM must match the physical form of the drug as supplied on the date of service — not how you prefer to bill it, and not what a prior claim used.
Why This Happens
⚠️
DATA ENTRY & SELECTION ISSUES
Most common root causes behind this denial
  • Wrong NDC selected for the drug actually administered or NDC from a different manufacturer was used
  • A 10-digit NDC was not converted to the required 11-digit (5-4-2) format
  • The HCPCS/J-code does not align with the NDC submitted
  • NDC is expired, discontinued, or no longer active in the payer's drug file
  • NDC field was blank — drug was billed by J-code only, no NDC attached
  • Drug purchased from a new supplier and NDC not captured at point of administration
How to Fix It
1
Coding Escalation - Step 1
Initial review and coding validation
Before Escalating, you must verify the NDC yourself first. Through www.aapc.com or Verify MR which Drug has been given to the Patient.
  • Checked the medical record — confirmed the drug name and NDC of what was actually given to the patient.
  • Confirmed the NDC is 11 digits with no dashes — and matches exactly what was submitted on the claim. CMS Box-24 & UB-04 FL-43-44
  • Looked up the NDC-to-HCPCS crosswalk — confirmed the billed HCPCS/CPT does not match the drug administered or NDC Expired.
2
Corrected Claim Resubmission - Step 2
Correct and resubmit the claim
Only do this after coding gives you the correct code or Verfied by CPC Coder NDC & UOB is Valid - Never resubmit without it or Always confirm about the Timely Filling.
  • Coding has confirmed and assigned the correct NDC or HCPCS/CPT code.
  • NDC is 11 digits, no dashes, with qualifier "N4" before it (example: N412345678901).
  • Unit of measure is included — UN (units), ML (milliliters), or GR (grams).
  • Still within the payer's resubmission deadline & Claim is marked as a corrected claim (frequency code 7 or as payer requires).
3
Calling Required - Step 3
Contact payer for clarification
This is a payer error. Call and ask them to reprocess the claim manually. Before this make everything Handy.
  • Have the original claim number, denial date, and corrected claim number ready.
  • Bring proof: NDC-to-HCPCS match, drug administration record, and the EOB.
  • Write down the rep's name, call reference number, and promised action.
4
Payer denied Reprocessing — Appeal required - Step 4
Escalate through the appeal process
If the payer refuses to reprocess and tells you to appeal — do this. Confrim Appeal TFL Days
  • Get the Denial | Appeal | Dispute letter or If there is no Specific Letter than make Cover Letter— note the appeal deadline (usually 30–180 days). Do not miss it.
  • Write an appeal letter stating: the correct NDC was billed, it matches the HCPCS/CPT, and the denial was incorrect.
  • Attach MR: drug administration record, physician order, NDC crosswalk, and original claim.
  • Send via Web Portal or Fax or Mailing or certified mail — keep a copy and the delivery confirmation.
  • Follow up after 30 days if no response received.
5
Payer says info not received on Form — Appeal required - Step 5
Verify placement of NDC information
The payer says they never received NDC details — even though you submitted them. Check these. CMS Box#23 & UB FL-43-44
  • On a paper claim (CMS-1500) — NDC must be in Box 24, shaded area.
  • On an electronic claim — NDC goes in the LIN/CTP segment.
  • Unit of measure must be included — UN, ML, or GR.
  • If all fields look correct — call the payer and ask exactly which field they require for NDC. Some payers have custom rules.
  • Resubmit with the correction and confirm the payer received it.
Modifier to Know
🏷️
Modifier Reference Guide
Common modifiers and proper usage guidelines
Modifier Description Usage Notes
KO Single drug unit dose formulation Use for single-drug unit-dose packaging.
KP First drug in a multiple drug unit dose formulation First drug when multiple drugs are in one unit-dose.
KQ Second or subsequent drug in a multiple drug unit dose formulation Each additional drug after KP in multi-drug unit-dose.
JW Drug amount discarded or not administered to the patient Requires a separate line on the same claim. Primary line must show >0 units administered. JW line shows wasted/discarded quantity. Both lines must reflect the same NDC and J-code.
JZ Zero wastage — no drug was discarded Use when no drug was discarded. Required by some payers when JW is not applicable.
💊
JW Modifier — Correct Billing Structure Example
Line 1
Line 1: J-code | 2 ML administered | same NDC | same DOS
Line 2
Line 2: J-code + JW | 0.5 ML discarded | same NDC | same DOS If the JW line is the only line, or if the administered line shows zero units, the claim will deny.
Important: If the JW line is the only line, or if the administered line shows zero units, the claim will deny.
WHAT NOT TO DO
🚫
What NOT to Do
Avoid these common actions that lead to repeat denials
  • Do not appeal before correcting the NDC/J-code mismatch
  • Do not reuse old, default, or copied NDCs from previous claims without verification
  • Do not guess at the manufacturer, quantity, or drug lot always Verify with MR
  • Do not submit a corrected claim with the same incorrect NDC
  • Do not appeal a N846 denial if you have not yet submitted a corrected claim first
⚠️
Critical Reminder
Appealing before correcting the underlying NDC error wastes timely filing days and may forfeit your right to correct the claim. Always correct first, then appeal only if the corrected claim is also denied.
PREVENTIVE CHECKLIST
✅
PREVENTIVE CHECKLIST
Pre-submission validation checklist

Before submitting any drug claim, confirm:

  • ✅ NDC captured directly from the administered vial or package label
  • ✅ J-code validated against NDC using a current, reliable crosswalk
  • ✅ 10-digit NDC converted to 11-digit format (5-4-2) if applicable
  • ✅ N4 qualifier formatted with no space before the NDC number
  • ✅ Manufacturer-specific NDC confirmed (not from a different lot or supplier)
  • ✅ JW modifier applied on a separate line with administered quantity on primary line (if wastage applies)
  • ✅ NDC confirmed as active on the date of service
  • ✅ Claim run through edits before submission
Best Practice — When to Appeal vs. When to Correct
✅

Appeal Only When ALL of These Are True

Use only when the claim has already been corrected and still denied
  • A corrected claim has already been submitted and denied again
  • All billing data (NDC, J-code, UOM, quantity, manufacturer) has been confirmed correct against the MAR and vial label
  • The denial reason after correction still cites N846 — indicating a payer-side processing error
🔄

Correct & Resubmit First (Most Cases)

Recommended first step for most denials
  • Most N846 denials resolve by correcting and resubmitting the claim
  • Do not appeal as a first step - Verify NDC ↔ J-code crosswalk, 11-digit format, N4 qualifier, UOM, and quantity before resubmission
  • Use Resubmission Code 7 on the corrected claim

M119 SUPPLEMENTAL CO-16

Missing / Deactivated / Withdrawn NDC

Drug Billing / Data Mismatch · Professional & Facility Claims ·
Common CARC Pairing
CMS-1500 Box 24 & UB-04 (FL43-FL44) CO-16 Claim/service lacks information
What This Means
💡
What This Means

M119 used by payers to flag that the (NDC) on the claim is one of the following: completely missing, incomplete (e.g., wrong digit count), formatted incorrectly, or the NDC has been deactivated or withdrawn from the FDA drug database.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
M119 covers four distinct problem types. Before taking any action, you must identify which of the four applies — each has a different resolution path. A missing NDC is a data entry fix. A withdrawn NDC may require a NOC code and clinical documentation. Treating all M119 denials the same way is the most common mistake.

What This Means

TOB = Facility Type + Care Type + Claim Frequency

• If the service happened at home, think "HHA"

  • Review the 837I file and confirm the TOB was present in Loop 2300 CLM05-1.
  • Include screenshots, clearinghouse acknowledgments, and claim images as proof.
  • Ask the payer to investigate whether a system intake issue removed the field.
  • Resubmit only after confirming the TOB field is transmitting correctly.
WHAT NOT TO DO
🚫
What NOT to Do
Avoid these common actions that lead to repeat denials
  • Do not resubmit the claim with the same invalid or incomplete TOB — it will deny again with MA30
  • Do not change the TOB without reviewing all cross-validated fields — a TOB correction that is not reconciled with revenue codes, admission date, discharge status, and occurrence codes will generate secondary denials
  • Do not use frequency code 7 (replacement) without a valid prior claim to replace — submitting a replacement against a claim that was never processed will create a new claim rather than replace the denied one
  • Do not use frequency code 8 (void) unless the original claim was paid and must be fully reversed — a void against an unpaid claim may not process correctly
  • Do not carry an incorrect TOB to secondary or tertiary claims — the wrong TOB on the primary will generate a cascade of secondary denials
⚠️
Critical Reminder
Appealing MA30 before correcting the TOB error wastes timely filing days and is almost never successful — the payer will uphold the denial because the claim data itself is incorrect. Correct the TOB, reconcile all cross-validated fields, and resubmit before considering an appeal.

MA40 REMARK CO-16

Missing / Incomplete / Invalid Admission Date

Facility Billing / UB-04 · Admission Date (FL12) · Inpatient & SNF
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 UB-04 FL12 Inpatient SNF
What This Means

The Admission Date field (Form Locator 12) on the UB-04 claim was either left blank, submitted with an invalid date format, or contains a date that conflicts with other fields on the claim. FL12 is required on all inpatient hospital and SNF claims — the payer cannot determine the start of the benefit period without it, and the claim will suspend or deny with MA40 until it is corrected.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
MA40 does not mean the stay is not covered. It is a data integrity error — the claim cannot be processed until a valid, correctly formatted admission date is present in FL12. This is almost always resolved with a corrected claim, not an appeal.

Where FL12 Is on the UB-04
UB-04 Form Locator 12
  • FL12 — Admission / Start of Care Date: The date the patient was admitted for an inpatient stay or the start of a home health or SNF episode
  • Format: MM/DD/YY (6-digit) per UB-04 instructions — some payers accept MMDDYYYY (8-digit) in the EDI 837I loop
  • EDI 837I equivalent: Loop 2300 DTP*435 (Admission Date)
  • Required on all inpatient (TOB 011x, 021x) and SNF claims
  • Not required on outpatient (TOB 013x) or HHA final claims (TOB 032x) unless payer-specific rules apply
Fields That Cross-Validate With FL12
  • FL6 — Statement Covers Period (From Date): Cannot be earlier than FL12 admission date
  • FL17 — Patient Discharge Status: Must be present if FL12 is present; discharge date must be on or after FL12
  • FL18–FL28 — Condition Codes: Certain condition codes are date-driven from the admission date
  • FL31–FL34 — Occurrence Codes / Dates: Occurrence codes tied to admission (e.g., OC 01 — Accident) must be on or after FL12
  • FL4 — Type of Bill: Inpatient TOB (011x, 021x) triggers FL12 requirement
  • FL67 — Principal Diagnosis: POA indicator validation is anchored to the admission date
Why This Happens — Most Common Causes
  • FL12 left blank during claim entry — most common cause
  • Admission date entered in wrong format (e.g., YYYY-MM-DD instead of MM/DD/YY)
  • Date entered as all zeros (000000) or with placeholder text
  • EHR-to-billing system interface failed to map the admit date to FL12
  • Admit date on the claim does not match ADT (Admission, Discharge, Transfer) system records
  • Admission date is after the statement period end date — impossible chronological sequence
  • Admission date is in the future — date entry error
  • Claim was copied from a prior patient encounter and the admission date was not updated
  • SNF claim submitted without the admission date from the qualifying hospital stay
  • Late charge claim (TOB frequency 5) submitted and the original admission date was not carried over
  • Interim continuing or interim last claim submitted without the original admission date from the interim first
How to Fix It
Standard Correction — Step by Step
  1. 1Pull the face sheet, ADT system record, or registration document to confirm the correct admission date
  2. 2Confirm the date format matches payer requirements — typically MM/DD/YY on the paper UB-04 or MMDDYYYY in the EDI 837I DTP*435 segment
  3. 3Check that the admission date is not later than the Statement From date (FL6) — if it is, also correct FL6
  4. 4Check that the admission date is not after the discharge date (FL17 / Statement Thru date)
  5. 5For SNF claims — verify the admission date reflects the SNF admission, not the prior qualifying hospital admission, unless payer-specific rules require the hospital admit date
  6. 6For interim continuing / interim last claims — carry the same original admission date from the interim first claim
  7. 7Add Condition Code D4 if correcting a previously submitted claim (indicates TOB or data correction)
  8. 8Submit a corrected claim using Frequency Code 7 (Replacement) in the Type of Bill
Condition Code D4 — When and Why to Use It
ℹ️
Always Include D4 on Corrected Claims
Condition Code D4 signals to the payer that this claim is a correction of a previously submitted claim. It is required when changing FL12 on a replacement claim (Frequency Code 7) to ensure the payer's system replaces — rather than duplicates — the original submission.
  • D4 goes in FL18–FL28 (Condition Code field) on the corrected UB-04
  • If omitted on a replacement claim, the payer may treat the corrected claim as a duplicate and deny it
  • D4 is required regardless of whether the only correction is the admission date or whether multiple fields are being corrected
What NOT to Do
  • Do not appeal before submitting a corrected claim with the valid admission date
  • Do not submit the corrected claim with the same blank or invalid FL12
  • Do not assume the admission date carried over correctly from a prior claim without verifying the actual FL12 value
  • Do not use the hospital admission date on an SNF claim if the SNF had a separate, later admission date — verify which date applies
  • Do not omit Condition Code D4 on the replacement claim
  • Do not carry forward a placeholder date (e.g., 01/01/00 or 000000) from a billing template
🚫
Appealing before correcting FL12 wastes timely filing days. MA40 is almost always a correctable data error — verify the admission date from the ADT system, correct it, and resubmit with Frequency Code 7 and Condition Code D4.
Prevention Checklist

Before submitting any inpatient or SNF claim, confirm:

  • ✅FL12 is populated — not blank, not zero-filled, not a placeholder
  • ✅Date format is MM/DD/YY (paper) or MMDDYYYY (EDI 837I)
  • ✅Admission date is confirmed against the face sheet or ADT system record
  • ✅Admission date is on or before the Statement From date (FL6)
  • ✅Admission date is on or before the discharge / Statement Thru date
  • ✅For interim claims — admission date matches the original admission date from the first claim in the series
  • ✅For corrected claims — Condition Code D4 is included and Frequency Code 7 is in the TOB
  • ✅Claim run through scrubber with FL12 validation before submission
Best Practice — When to Appeal vs. When to Correct

✅ Appeal Only When ALL of These Are True

  • A corrected claim with the verified admission date has already been submitted and denied again
  • The admission date on the corrected claim is confirmed correct against ADT system records
  • The continued denial is due to a payer-side system or date validation error — not a data issue on the claim

🔄 Correct & Resubmit First (Most Cases)

  • The vast majority of MA40 denials resolve by populating or correcting FL12 and resubmitting
  • Do not appeal as a first step — confirm the admission date from the ADT or face sheet first
  • Use Frequency Code 7 and Condition Code D4 on the corrected claim

M44 REMARK CO-16

Missing / Incomplete / Invalid Condition Code

Facility Billing / UB-04 · Condition Codes (FL18–FL28) · Inpatient & Outpatient
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 UB-04 FL18–FL28 Inpatient Outpatient SNF
What This Means

Condition Codes (Form Locators 18–28 on the UB-04) provide supplemental claim-level information that the payer needs to correctly adjudicate the claim — such as whether the claim is a correction, whether the patient had other insurance, whether an accident was involved, or whether a specific regulatory or billing exception applies. M44 is triggered when a required condition code is missing, an invalid code was submitted, or the code submitted is inconsistent with other claim fields. The claim cannot process correctly until the condition code issue is resolved.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
M44 does not mean the services are not covered. It means the payer's edit system detected a condition code that is missing, invalid, or conflicts with another field on the claim. In most cases this is resolved with a corrected claim — not an appeal. The specific condition code required depends entirely on the claim scenario, payer, and TOB.

Where Condition Codes Live on the UB-04
UB-04 Form Locators FL18–FL28
  • FL18–FL28: Up to 11 condition codes can be reported on a single claim — each FL holds one 2-character alphanumeric code
  • Condition codes are always exactly 2 characters — never 1, never 3
  • EDI 837I equivalent: Loop 2300 HI segment with qualifier BG (Condition Code)
  • Codes are defined in the NUBC UB-04 Data Specifications Manual
  • Certain codes are required by regulation for specific claim types (e.g., D4 for corrected claims, 04 for information-only claims)
  • Some payers have proprietary condition code requirements beyond the standard NUBC set — always check payer-specific billing guidelines
Fields That Cross-Validate With Condition Codes
  • FL4 — Type of Bill: Some condition codes are TOB-specific (e.g., D4 required when correcting FL4)
  • FL12 — Admission Date: Accident-related codes (OC 01, OC 02) must have occurrence dates on or after admission
  • FL38 — Responsible Party / Guarantor: MSP-related condition codes must align with coordination of benefits data
  • FL50 — Payer Name: MSP codes must be consistent with the payer listed in FL50
  • FL58–FL63 — Insured Info: Insurance-related condition codes must align with FL58–FL63
  • FL17 — Discharge Status: Hospice or SNF condition codes must be consistent with the discharge status
  • FL66 — DRG / Principal Dx: Some condition codes interact with DRG assignment edits

Why This Happens — Most Common Causes
  • Required condition code omitted — most common cause (e.g., D4 missing on a corrected claim)
  • Invalid 2-digit code entered — typo, extra character, or code does not exist in the NUBC code set
  • Condition code is mutually exclusive with another code already on the claim (e.g., two conflicting MSP codes)
  • MSP-related code missing when payer records show another primary payer exists
  • Condition code required by payer-specific policy not included (e.g., payer requires code 20 for ABN billing)

  • Code submitted does not apply to the TOB on the claim (e.g., hospice-specific code on an inpatient hospital claim)
  • EHR-to-billing system failed to map a required condition code to FL18–FL28
  • Billing template carries a stale or incorrect default condition code not appropriate for the current claim
  • Code submitted is a retired or invalid NUBC condition code no longer accepted by the payer
  • Payer requires a condition code that was added by a recent policy change the billing team was not notified of

M34 REMARK CO-16

Claim Lacks the CLIA Certification Number

Laboratory Billing · CMS-1500 Box 23 / UB-04 FL81 · Professional & Facility Lab Claims
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 CMS-1500 UB-04 Box 23 Lab Claims CPT 80000–89999
What This Means

Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments. Any facility — independent lab, physician office lab, hospital outreach lab, or clinic — that performs testing on human specimens for health assessment, diagnosis, treatment, or prevention of disease is required by federal law to hold a current CLIA certificate. When a claim includes laboratory procedure codes (typically CPT 80000–89999) and the CLIA certification number is missing, incomplete, or invalid, the payer will deny with M34. The claim cannot process until a valid CLIA number is present on the claim.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
M34 does not mean the lab tests are not covered or medically unnecessary. It is a regulatory compliance and data integrity error — the payer cannot confirm that the performing laboratory is federally certified to conduct the tests billed. This is almost always resolved with a corrected claim supplying the valid CLIA number. Do not appeal before correcting the claim.

What Is a CLIA Number & What Does It Look Like
CLIA Number Format
  • CLIA numbers are exactly 10 characters — no more, no fewer
  • Format: 2-letter state code + D + 7 numeric digits
  • Example: 14D0000001 — where 14 = state code, D = laboratory type designator, 0000001 = sequential number
  • The letter D in position 3 is the standard lab designator — some specialty labs use different letters (e.g., H for HHS/reference labs)
  • No dashes, spaces, or special characters — entered as a continuous 10-character string
  • CLIA numbers are issued and maintained by CMS — searchable in the CLIA Online Database (QCOR)
CLIA Number Format: ✅ Correct: 14D0000001 (10 chars, no spaces) ❌ Wrong: 14D-000-0001 (dashes not allowed) ❌ Wrong: 14D00001 (too short — must be 10) ❌ Wrong: 14D00000001X (too long — must be 10) ❌ Wrong: 14D0000000 (invalid check digit)
Types of CLIA Certificates
  • Certificate of Waiver (CoW): Labs performing only CLIA-waived simple tests (e.g., dipstick UA, fingerstick glucose, rapid strep). Limited test menu — waived tests only
  • Certificate for Provider-Performed Microscopy (PPM): Physician or mid-level providers who perform microscopy procedures during the patient visit (e.g., wet mounts, KOH preps)
  • Certificate of Compliance (CoC): Labs performing moderate or high-complexity testing that pass a standard CLIA inspection
  • Certificate of Accreditation (CoA): Labs accredited by a CMS-approved accreditation organization (CAP, Joint Commission, COLA, AABB, etc.) — deemed to meet CLIA requirements
  • Certificate of Registration: Temporary certificate issued to new labs while awaiting inspection — allows billing during the initial certification period
⚠️
Certificate Type Must Match Tests Billed
A Certificate of Waiver only covers waived tests. Billing high-complexity CPT codes under a CoW CLIA number is a compliance violation and will result in M34 or a medical necessity/compliance denial. Always confirm the certificate type covers the complexity level of the tests being billed.
Where the CLIA Number Goes on the Claim
CMS-1500 (Professional Claims)
  • Box 23 — Prior Authorization Number: The CLIA certification number is entered in Box 23 when billing laboratory services on the CMS-1500
  • Enter the 10-character CLIA number with no spaces, dashes, or qualifiers in the printed Box 23 field
  • EDI 837P equivalent: Loop 2300 REF*X4 — qualifier X4 with the 10-digit CLIA number as the reference identification
  • The CLIA number in Box 23 identifies the performing laboratory — even if the ordering physician is billing
  • If the performing lab is a reference lab (send-out), the reference lab's CLIA number goes in Box 23, not the ordering provider's CLIA

  • EDI 837I equivalent: Loop 2300 REF*X4 — same qualifier and format as the 837P
  • For hospital outpatient lab claims billed under the hospital's CLIA, use the hospital's CLIA number
  • For reference lab send-outs billed on a UB-04, use the reference lab's CLIA number

Box 23 on the CMS-1500 is a shared field used for both prior authorization numbers and CLIA numbers depending on the service type. On laboratory claims, Box 23 must carry the CLIA number — not a prior auth number. If a prior auth is also required for the lab test, check the payer's companion guide for how to submit both — some payers use a separate loop or note field.

A Certificate of Waiver only covers the specific waived tests on the FDA's waived test list. Why This Happens — Most Common Causes
  • Box 23 (CMS-1500) or FL81 (UB-04) left blank during claim entry — most common cause
  • Billing system not configured to auto-populate the CLIA number on lab claim lines
  • CLIA number expired — laboratory's certificate lapsed and was not renewed before the date of service
  • Wrong CLIA number submitted — number belongs to a different location, a different lab, or contains a typo
  • CLIA number entered with incorrect format — dashes, spaces, or wrong character count
  • Reference lab's CLIA not included — ordering provider billed for a send-out test but did not populate the performing reference lab's CLIA number
  • Multi-site practice used the corporate or headquarters CLIA instead of the site-specific CLIA for the location that actually performed the test
  • New lab or new physician office lab — CLIA application submitted but certificate not yet issued; billing started before CLIA was in hand
  • EHR-to-billing interface does not map the CLIA number to Box 23 or FL81 in the claim export
  • Provider billed a prior auth number in Box 23 (overwriting the CLIA field) without realizing the field serves dual purpose
  • Claim template carries a CLIA number for a different provider or expired certificate and was not updated
  • Payer updated their CLIA validation rules and now requires the number on claim types where it was previously optional
How to Fix It — Step by Step
Standard Correction Process
  1. 1Identify which lab performed the testing on the date of service — the CLIA number must belong to the performing lab, not the ordering provider
  2. 2Locate the performing lab's CLIA certificate — check the physical certificate on file, the practice management system's provider setup, or look up the CLIA number in the CMS CLIA Online Database (QCOR) at qcor.cms.gov
  3. 3Verify the CLIA certificate was active and not expired on the date of service — an expired CLIA will deny even if the number format is correct
  4. 4Verify the CLIA certificate type covers the complexity level of the CPT codes being billed (waived vs. moderate vs. high complexity)
  5. 5Confirm the CLIA number format — exactly 10 characters, no dashes or spaces
  6. 6Enter the CLIA number in the correct claim field: Box 23 on CMS-1500 or FL81 with X4 qualifier on UB-04
  7. 7Verify no other data is conflicting with Box 23 — if a prior auth is also needed, check the payer companion guide for how to handle both
  8. 8Run the corrected claim through the scrubber to confirm the CLIA field validates before submission
  9. 9Submit the corrected claim with Resubmission Code 7 (replacement claim)
🔍
How to Look Up a CLIA Number
Go to qcor.cms.gov - Right Side Quick Access Tools Search CLIA → Basic Search → search by provider name, address, or NPI. The result will show the CLIA number, certificate type, effective date, expiration date, and certificate status. Always confirm the certificate was active on the date of service before billing.
CLIA Expiration, Renewal & What To Do If the Certificate Lapsed
CLIA Certificate Validity
  • Most CLIA certificates are valid for 2 years from the date of issue
  • Certificate of Waiver — renewed every 2 years
  • Certificate of Compliance and Accreditation — renewed every 2 years following successful inspection or accreditation renewal
  • CMS sends renewal notices approximately 6 months before expiration — do not wait for the notice; track the expiration date proactively
  • Billing for lab services after the CLIA expiration date is a federal compliance violation — claims will deny and may trigger an audit
If the Certificate Expired Before DOS
  • Do not submit a corrected claim with the expired CLIA number — the payer will deny again
  • Contact your CMS Regional Office or CLIA program contractor immediately to determine if retroactive reinstatement is possible
  • In some cases, CMS will issue a Certificate of Registration while the renewal inspection is scheduled — confirm if this covers the DOS
  • If services were billed under an expired CLIA, escalate to compliance before resubmitting — this may require a refund or repayment to Medicare
  • Document all steps taken and keep correspondence with CMS on file
If the Certificate Was Valid But Payer Still Denies
  • Call the payer's provider services and confirm the CLIA number is loaded in their provider file
  • Some payers maintain their own internal CLIA validation database — if the lab's CLIA is not in their system, provide the CLIA certificate as supporting documentation
  • Request that the payer update their provider file with the current CLIA number before resubmitting the corrected claim
  • If the payer confirms the CLIA is valid but the claim still denies, escalate to a first-level appeal with a copy of the CLIA certificate attached

🔬

For waived tests, modifier QW and a valid Certificate of Waiver CLIA number must both be present. If either is missing, the claim will deny. QW without a CoW CLIA number triggers M34. A CoW CLIA without QW may cause the payer's system to expect a higher-complexity certificate and deny for the wrong certificate type. Always submit both together on every waived test line.
What NOT to Do
  • Do not appeal before correcting the CLIA number on the claim — M34 is almost always a data correction, not a coverage dispute
  • Do not submit a corrected claim with the same blank, invalid, or expired CLIA number
  • Do not use the ordering physician's CLIA number on a reference lab send-out — the performing lab's CLIA must be used
  • Do not use a corporate or headquarters CLIA number for a satellite testing location that has its own site-specific CLIA
  • Do not begin billing laboratory services before the CLIA certificate is issued — a CLIA application in process is not a valid CLIA number
  • Do not enter dashes, spaces, or extra characters in the CLIA number field — exactly 10 characters, no formatting
  • Do not assume the billing system auto-populates Box 23 correctly — verify the CLIA number in the claim preview before submitting
  • Do not submit a Certificate of Waiver CLIA number for tests that require moderate or high-complexity certification
  • Do not put a prior authorization number in Box 23 without confirming that it does not overwrite the CLIA number requirement for lab claims
🚫
Billing laboratory services without a valid, active CLIA number is not only a claim denial — it is a federal regulatory violation under CLIA (42 CFR Part 493). Repeated billing under an expired or invalid CLIA can trigger a CMS audit, civil monetary penalties, and exclusion from Medicare and Medicaid. Always verify CLIA status before billing.
Prevention Checklist

Before submitting any laboratory claim, confirm:

  • ✅CLIA number is populated in Box 23 (CMS-1500) or FL81 with X4 qualifier (UB-04) — not blank
  • ✅CLIA number belongs to the performing laboratory — not the ordering provider
  • ✅CLIA number is exactly 10 characters with no dashes, spaces, or special characters
  • ✅CLIA certificate was active and not expired on the date of service — verified in QCOR
  • ✅CLIA certificate type (CoW, CoC, CoA) covers the complexity level of the CPT codes being billed
  • ✅For waived tests — Modifier QW appended to each waived CPT code AND a CoW CLIA number is in Box 23
  • ✅For reference lab send-outs — Modifier 90 is on the claim line AND the reference lab's CLIA is in Box 23
  • ✅For multi-site practices — site-specific CLIA used for the location that actually performed the test
  • ✅Billing system configured to auto-populate the correct CLIA number — verified in claim preview before submission
  • ✅CLIA expiration date tracked — renewal initiated well before expiration; no billing gap during renewal period
  • ✅Payer companion guide reviewed — payer does not require CLIA to be pre-loaded in their provider portal
  • ✅Claim run through scrubber with CLIA field validation before submission
Best Practice — When to Appeal vs. When to Correct

✅ Appeal Only When ALL of These Are True

  • A corrected claim with the valid, active CLIA number has already been submitted and denied again
  • The CLIA number is confirmed active on the date of service via QCOR lookup
  • The CLIA certificate type covers the complexity of the tests billed
  • The payer has confirmed via provider services that the denial is a system-side validation or provider file issue — not a problem with the claim data
  • A copy of the CLIA certificate is available to attach to the appeal as supporting documentation

🔄 Correct & Resubmit First (Most Cases)

  • The vast majority of M34 denials resolve by populating or correcting the CLIA number in Box 23 or FL81 and resubmitting
  • Do not appeal as a first step — verify the CLIA number via QCOR and correct the claim first
  • Use Resubmission Code 7 on the corrected claim
  • If the CLIA was expired on the DOS — do not resubmit without escalating to compliance first

Facility Billing / UB-04 · Occurrence Codes (FL31–FL34) · Inpatient & Outpatient
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 UB-04 FL31–FL34 Inpatient Outpatient SNF HHA
What This Means

Occurrence Codes on the UB-04 (Form Locators 31–34) are two-digit codes paired with a date that document specific events, accidents, or conditions related to the patient's claim — such as the date of an accident, the onset of symptoms, the date Medicare was notified of a hospice election, or the start of a home health episode. The claim cannot process correctly until the occurrence code issue is resolved.

ℹ️
Important Distinction
M45 does not mean the services are not covered. It is a data integrity error — the payer's edit system flagged a missing, invalid, or conflicting occurrence code that is required to correctly adjudicate the claim. In nearly all cases this is resolved with a corrected claim. The specific occurrence code required depends entirely on the clinical scenario, payer, and Type of Bill.

Where Occurrence Codes Live on the UB-04
UB-04 Form Locators FL31–FL34
  • FL31–FL34: Each field holds one occurrence code + date pair — up to 4 occurrence codes per claim
  • Each occurrence code is exactly 2 characters (alphanumeric)
  • Each code must be paired with a date in MM/DD/YY format — a code without a date, or a date without a code, will trigger M45
  • EDI 837I equivalent: Loop 2300 HI segment with qualifier BH (Occurrence Code) — format: HI*BH:code:date~
  • Codes are defined in the NUBC UB-04 Data Specifications Manual
  • Order of occurrence codes on the claim does not matter — all open FL31–FL34 fields can be used in any sequence
  • If more than 4 occurrence codes are needed, use FL35–FL36 (Occurrence Span Codes) for date-range events
Fields That Cross-Validate With Occurrence Codes
  • FL4 — Type of Bill: Some occurrence codes are TOB-specific — e.g., accident codes (01–04) required when TOB indicates accident-related billing
  • FL12 — Admission Date: Occurrence code dates for events that precede admission (e.g., accident date) must be on or before the admission date where clinically appropriate
  • FL6 — Statement Covers Period: Occurrence dates must fall within or before the statement period unless they document a pre-admission event
  • FL18–FL28 — Condition Codes: MSP-related occurrence codes (e.g., OC 24 for auto accident) must align with MSP condition codes
  • FL50 — Payer Name / FL38 — Responsible Party: Accident-related occurrence codes must be consistent with listed primary payer for MSP coordination
  • FL17 — Discharge Status: Hospice occurrence codes must align with discharge status and hospice TOB
  • FL35–FL36 — Occurrence Span Codes: Span codes cover date ranges — occurrence codes (FL31–FL34) cover single dates; using the wrong type triggers additional edits
UB-04 Occurrence Code Entry Format: FL31: Code | Date Example: 01 | 03/14/25 (Auto accident date) FL32: Code | Date Example: 11 | 03/14/25 (Onset of symptoms) FL33: Code | Date Example: 50 | 03/14/25 (Medicare Part A exhausted) FL34: Code | Date (Leave blank if no fourth code needed) EDI 837I Format: HI*BH:01:20250314*BH:11:20250314*BH:50:20250314~

Submitting a span event as a point-in-time code — or vice versa — will trigger M45 or a separate span code error. Always use the correct type for the event being documented.

  • Occurrence date is outside the valid range — date after the statement period end date or before a plausible clinical event
  • Invalid occurrence code submitted — code does not exist in the current NUBC code set, contains a typo, or is a retired code
  • Wrong occurrence code used for the event — e.g., using an occurrence span code number in the occurrence code field
  • Accident-related codes missing on claims where diagnosis codes indicate injury or trauma (ICD-10 V, W, X, Y codes present)
  • Hospice claims missing OC 27 (date of certification) or OC 51 (date of hospice election)
  • SNF claims missing OC 70 (qualifying stay dates) or occurrence span code for qualifying hospital stay
  • Therapy claims missing plan of care dates (OC 17, 18, 19, 35, 44, 45)
  • EHR-to-billing system interface does not map occurrence codes and dates to FL31–FL34
  • Billing template carries stale or inapplicable default occurrence codes not updated for the current claim
  • Payer updated occurrence code requirements and the billing team was not notified
How to Fix It — Step by Step
Standard Correction Process
  1. 1Pull the original EOB or remittance — confirm M45 is the denial reason and identify whether the issue is a missing code, invalid code, missing date, or conflicting data
  2. 2Review the claim scenario against the M45 reference table above — identify which occurrence code(s) are required for the TOB, clinical situation, and payer
  3. 3Confirm the correct date to pair with each code — pull source documentation (face sheet, accident report, therapy plan, hospice certification, discharge summary) to verify the date
  4. 4Check whether an occurrence code or an occurrence span code is the correct type for the event — single date = FL31–FL34; date range = FL35–FL36
  5. 5Verify the date format — MM/DD/YY on the paper UB-04; MMDDYYYY in the EDI 837I HI*BH segment
  6. 6Confirm no conflicting occurrence codes are present — remove or replace any code that is mutually exclusive or contradicts other claim fields
  7. 7Check payer-specific companion guide — some payers require occurrence codes beyond the standard NUBC set or have specific date rules
  8. 8Add Condition Code D4 to FL18–FL28 and use Frequency Code 7 in the TOB to indicate this is a corrected replacement claim
  9. 9Run the corrected claim through the scrubber with occurrence code validation active before resubmission
  10. 10Submit the corrected claim with Frequency Code 7 (Replacement) in the Type of Bill
⚠️
Call the Payer If the Required Code Is Unclear
If the EOB does not specify which occurrence code is missing or why the submitted code was rejected, call the payer's provider services line before resubmitting. Ask specifically which code and date their system requires for this claim type and scenario — this prevents repeated denials from guessing the wrong code.

M62 REMARK CO-197

Missing Treatment Authorization Code

Prior Authorization / Pre-Certification · Professional & Facility Claims · CMS-1500 & UB-04
Common CARC Pairing
CO-197 CO-15 CMS-1500 UB-04 Box 23 FL63
What This Means

M62 means the payer's system could not find a valid, matching treatment authorization (also called a Prior Authorization or Pre-Auth) number on the claim. Either the authorization number was not submitted at all, it was entered incorrectly, it belongs to a different patient or service, it has expired, or the service billed does not match what was actually authorized. The payer cannot pay the claim until it can verify that the service was approved in advance.

ℹ️
Think of it this way — What is a Prior Authorization?
Before certain procedures, tests, or specialist visits happen, the insurance company requires the provider to ask for permission in advance. That "permission" is called a Prior Authorization (PA). The payer gives back an authorization number — a unique code that proves the service was approved. That number must be reported on the claim in a specific field. M62 fires when that number is missing, wrong, or does not match what the payer approved.
⚠️
M62 Is NOT Always a Billing Error
Sometimes the authorization number is correct but the payer's system has a data entry issue on their end, or the authorization was not properly linked to the claim. Always confirm the auth number before assuming it is a billing mistake.
Where the Authorization Number Goes on the Claim Form
CMS-1500 (Professional / Office Claims)
  • Box 23 — Prior Authorization Number: This is the dedicated field for the PA number on the CMS-1500. It must contain only the authorization number — no extra text, no spaces at the start or end.
  • The auth number must match exactly what the payer issued — including any letters, leading zeros, or dashes.
  • If the service spans multiple dates, confirm whether the payer requires a new auth number per date of service or per authorization period.
  • Box 24B — Place of Service: The location code (POS) billed must match the setting that was authorized. Example: if auth was approved for outpatient office (POS 11) but you bill inpatient (POS 21), it will not match.
  • Box 24D — Procedure Code: The CPT/HCPCS code billed must match the procedure that was authorized — billing a different code, even a related one, will cause M62.
UB-04 (Facility / Hospital Claims)
  • FL63 — Treatment Authorization Code: This is the field for the prior authorization number on the UB-04. Each payer may have slightly different formatting expectations — always check the payer's billing companion guide.
  • FL4 — Type of Bill (TOB): The TOB must be consistent with the service setting that was authorized. A mismatch between TOB and the authorized setting will trigger M62.
  • FL42/FL44 — Revenue Code / Procedure Code: The procedure and revenue codes billed must align with what was specifically approved in the authorization.
  • FL67 — Principal Diagnosis: Some payers tie the authorization to a specific diagnosis — confirm the primary DX on the claim matches the DX used when the auth was obtained.
✅
Quick tip: The authorization number is case-sensitive for many payers. If the payer issued AUTH-2024-88341 and you entered auth2024-88341, it will not match — copy it exactly.
Why This Happens — Most Common Root Causes
Data Entry & Submission Errors
  • Authorization number was never entered in Box 23 (CMS-1500) or FL63 (UB-04) — the field was simply left blank
  • Authorization number was typed incorrectly — a digit transposed, a letter missing, extra spaces, or incorrect capitalization
  • The auth number from a previous claim or a different patient was accidentally copied onto this claim
  • The authorization was obtained but never communicated from the clinical or scheduling team to the billing team before claim submission
Authorization Validity Issues
  • The authorization number is valid but it has expired — the date of service falls outside the authorization's approved date range
  • The authorization was approved for a different procedure code than what was billed — even one digit off causes a mismatch
  • The authorization was approved for a different number of units or visits than what was billed
  • The authorization was issued under a different provider NPI — the rendering or billing provider on the claim does not match the provider on the authorization
  • The authorization was approved for a different place of service — e.g., authorized for outpatient but claim billed as inpatient
  • The service was performed by a different provider than who obtained the authorization (e.g., covering provider, locum)
System & Process Errors
  • The payer's system had a processing issue and did not correctly link the authorization to the claim even though it was submitted correctly — this requires a call to the payer
  • Authorization was voided or cancelled by the payer before the date of service without the provider being notified
How to Fix It — Step-by-Step
Step 1 — Pull the Authorization Record
  1. 1Go to the payer's provider portal or call the payer's provider services line and look up the authorization for this patient, date of service, and procedure code
  2. 2Confirm the authorization exists and is active — check the approved date range, approved procedure code(s), approved units, rendering provider NPI, and place of service
  3. 3Compare the authorization record to what was submitted on the claim — note any differences
Step 2 — Identify the Specific Mismatch
  1. 4Check Box 23 (CMS-1500) or FL63 (UB-04) on the original claim — was the auth number present? Was it entered correctly character by character?
  2. 5Confirm the procedure code, place of service, provider NPI, and dates of service on the claim all match the authorization exactly
  3. 6If the auth number was correct and all fields match, escalate to the payer — this may be a payer-side system issue requiring a reprocessing request
Step 3 — Correct and Resubmit
  1. 7Correct the auth number, procedure code, provider, or place of service as needed — then submit a corrected claim with Resubmission Code 7
  2. 8If the authorization itself was never obtained, contact the clinical or authorization team immediately — a retroactive authorization request may be possible depending on the payer and situation
  3. 9If the service date is outside the auth period, request an authorization extension from the payer before resubmitting
⚠️
Timely Filing Warning
While resolving an M62 denial, keep an eye on your timely filing deadline. Corrected claims and appeals still need to be submitted within the payer's filing window. Do not let the auth research delay the resubmission past the deadline.

  • ✅Authorization team confirmed auth approval and communicated the number to billing before claim submission

✅ Appeal When ALL of These Are True

  • A valid authorization was obtained before the date of service and the number was submitted correctly on the original claim
  • You have verified via the payer portal that the auth was active, matched the procedure, provider, and dates — and the claim data was correct
  • The payer denied it anyway — this indicates a payer-side system error and an appeal with the authorization approval documentation should be submitted
  • Always attach the authorization approval letter or portal screenshot to the appeal

🔄 Correct & Resubmit First (Most M62 Cases)

  • Most M62 denials are caused by a missing or incorrect auth number in Box 23 or FL63 — fix the field and resubmit with Resubmission Code 7
  • If the service was provided but no auth was ever obtained, work with the clinical team on a retroactive auth request before resubmitting
  • If the auth has expired, request an extension from the payer and resubmit once approved
  • Do not appeal as a first step — correct the root cause first and escalate to appeal only after a corrected claim is also denied

N4 REMARK CO-16

Missing Prior Insurance Carrier(s) Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

Coordination of Benefits (COB) · Secondary & Tertiary Billing · CMS-1500 & UB-04
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 CO-22 COB CMS-1500 UB-04 Box 11 FL50–FL65
What This Means

N4 means the secondary or tertiary payer (the insurance company you are currently billing) is saying: "We can see this patient has other insurance. Before we pay our share, we need to see proof of what that other insurance already paid — or decided not to pay — and why." That proof is called an Explanation of Benefits (EOB), also known as a Remittance Advice (RA). The EOB from the primary payer was either missing from the claim, was attached but had incomplete information, or the data entered in the COB fields on the claim did not match what the secondary payer expected to see.

ℹ️
Key Concept — What is Coordination of Benefits (COB)?
When a patient has more than one insurance plan, the plans have rules about who pays first (primary), who pays second (secondary), and who pays third (tertiary). This process is called Coordination of Benefits (COB). The secondary payer will only process the claim after the primary payer has already made a decision. The EOB from the primary payer is the document that tells the secondary payer: what was billed, what the primary payer allowed, what the primary payer paid, what adjustments were made, and what patient responsibility remains. Without that complete EOB data, the secondary payer cannot calculate their payment obligation — so they deny with N4.
⚠️
N4 Is Almost Always Fixable — It Is a Data Issue, Not a Coverage Issue
N4 does not mean the secondary insurance will not pay. It means the claim is on hold because the COB information is missing or does not match. Correct the EOB data on the claim and resubmit — in most cases the secondary will process and pay their portion.
Where to Report the Primary EOB Data on the Claim Form
CMS-1500 (Professional Claims)
  • Box 11 — Insured's Policy Group or FECA Number: Enter the primary insured's policy/group number — leaving this blank tells the secondary payer nothing about the primary plan
  • Box 11a — Insured's Date of Birth & Sex: Must match the primary insured's demographics exactly
  • Box 11b — Other Claim ID (Designated by NUCC): Used to report the primary payer's claim number or reference number from their EOB
  • Box 11c — Insurance Plan Name or Program Name: Enter the name of the primary insurance plan exactly as it appears on the EOB or insurance card
  • Box 11d — Is There Another Health Benefit Plan?: Must be checked YES if a secondary plan exists — if checked but COB fields are blank, N4 fires
  • Box 29 — Amount Paid: Enter the exact dollar amount the primary payer paid — this is the most critical field for COB and the most common source of N4 denials
✅
Critical: Box 29 must show the primary's paid amount in dollars — not zero unless the primary truly paid $0.00. A blank Box 29 or an incorrect amount is the single most common cause of N4 on CMS-1500 claims.
UB-04 (Facility / Hospital Claims)
  • FL50 — Payer Name (Lines A/B/C): Line A = primary payer, Line B = secondary payer, Line C = tertiary payer — all active payers must be listed in the correct order
  • FL51 — Health Plan ID: The payer identification number for each payer listed in FL50 — must be a valid payer ID recognized by the clearinghouse
  • FL54 — Prior Payments: Enter the amount the primary (and any other prior payer) already paid — this is the UB-04 equivalent of CMS-1500 Box 29 and is equally critical
  • FL56 — NPI: Billing provider NPI must be consistent with the provider recognized by the primary payer
  • FL58–FL63 — Insured Information: Name, relationship, and insurance ID for each payer must be complete and accurate — mismatches between FL58–63 and the primary EOB trigger N4
  • FL64 — Document Control Number: Enter the primary payer's claim number or document control number from the EOB — helps the secondary payer cross-reference the primary's payment record
  • FL65 — Employer Name: If the primary coverage is employer-sponsored, this field must be populated — required for MSP (Medicare Secondary Payer) situations
✅
Critical: FL54 (Prior Payments) must reflect the exact dollar amount paid by the primary. A blank FL54 or an amount that does not match the primary EOB is the most frequent source of N4 on UB-04 claims.
Why This Happens — Most Common Root Causes
Missing or Incomplete EOB Data on the Claim
  • Box 29 (CMS-1500) or FL54 (UB-04) was left blank — the primary payer's paid amount was never entered on the claim
  • The primary EOB was received but billing staff did not transfer the COB data into the claim fields before submitting to the secondary
  • The claim was submitted to the secondary before the primary had finished processing — the primary EOB had not yet been received
  • The claim was billed directly to the secondary without first submitting to the primary — always bill primary first unless the primary is known to be non-covered
Incorrect or Mismatched EOB Data
  • The paid amount entered in Box 29 or FL54 does not match the actual amount shown on the primary EOB — even a one-cent discrepancy can trigger N4
  • The primary payer name or ID entered on the claim does not match what the secondary payer has in their system for that carrier
  • The wrong EOB was attached — a different patient's or different date of service's remittance was used by mistake
  • The primary EOB shows a denial or zero payment but the reason was not communicated correctly on the secondary claim — the secondary needs to know why the primary paid nothing
COB Order & Coverage Issues
  • The payer order is incorrect — the plan listed as primary should actually be secondary, or vice versa (Birthday Rule, COBRA rules, or MSP rules were not applied correctly)
  • The patient's primary insurance has terminated but the claim was still sent as a COB claim — the secondary needs updated coverage information
  • Medicare is secondary (MSP situation) but the employer or group health plan did not process first — the MSP rules were not followed before billing Medicare
How to Fix It — Step-by-Step
Step 1 — Locate the Primary Payer's EOB
  1. 1Pull the primary payer's EOB or Electronic Remittance Advice (ERA) for this claim — confirm it is for the correct patient, date of service, and service line
  2. 2If the primary EOB has not been received yet — do not submit to the secondary. Wait until the primary has fully adjudicated the claim before billing secondary
  3. 3If the primary denied the claim entirely, obtain the denial EOB — you still need to submit it to the secondary with the denial reason so the secondary can determine their liability
Step 2 — Transfer All EOB Data to the Claim
  1. 4Enter the primary's paid amount exactly as shown on the EOB into Box 29 (CMS-1500) or FL54 (UB-04) — verify the dollar figure to the cent
  2. 5Confirm the primary payer name (Box 11c / FL50) and payer ID (FL51) are correct and match what the secondary recognizes for that carrier
  3. 6Verify the insured information fields (Box 11a–11d / FL58–FL63) match the primary insurance card and EOB exactly — name spelling, member ID, group number
  4. 7Enter the primary payer's claim number or document control number into Box 11b (CMS-1500) or FL64 (UB-04)
Step 3 — Correct & Resubmit
  1. 8Submit a corrected claim with Resubmission Code 7 to the secondary payer with all COB fields fully populated from the primary EOB
  2. 9If the secondary payer requires a paper EOB attached, include a clean copy of the primary EOB with the corrected claim — many secondary payers and Medicaid plans require this
  3. 10If the COB order was wrong (wrong primary vs. secondary), correct the payer order and resubmit to the correct payer first before billing the other
⚠️
Timely Filing — Both Payers Have Separate Deadlines
The secondary payer's timely filing clock typically starts from the primary payer's payment date — not the date of service. Always check the secondary payer's timely filing policy. Some payers allow 12 months from the primary EOB date; others allow only 90 days. Do not delay COB resubmissions.
Special Situation — Medicare as Secondary Payer (MSP)

MSP is one of the most common N4 scenarios in Medicare billing. When Medicare is the secondary payer, very specific rules and fields apply beyond standard COB.

MSP Situation Who Pays Primary Key Rule to Follow Common N4 Trigger
Working Aged Employer Group Health Plan (GHP) Patient is 65+ and still actively employed — employer GHP pays primary, Medicare pays secondary Claim sent to Medicare first without billing the GHP — Medicare denies with MSP-related RARC including N4

🚫

Billing Medicare as primary when another payer should be primary is not just a billing error — it can trigger a Medicare Secondary Payer Compliance Contractor (MSPCC) audit and a demand for repayment of any Medicare payments made in error. Always verify MSP status through Medicare's HIMR/HIQA system or CMS eligibility portal before billing Medicare on any patient with potential other coverage.
What NOT to Do
  • Do not submit to the secondary payer before the primary payer has fully processed and issued an EOB — the secondary will deny with N4 every time
  • Do not leave Box 29 (CMS-1500) or FL54 (UB-04) blank or enter $0.00 unless the primary truly paid nothing and you have the denial EOB to prove it
  • Do not estimate or approximate the primary paid amount — copy the exact dollar figure from the primary EOB, to the cent
  • Do not appeal N4 before correcting and resubmitting the claim with proper COB data — an appeal without the complete primary EOB information will be denied again
  • Do not assume the clearinghouse automatically forwards the primary EOB to the secondary — in most cases the billing team must manually populate the COB fields from the primary remittance
  • Do not ignore MSP rules — if another payer should be primary, billing Medicare or Medicaid first creates compliance risk beyond just a denial
🚫
The most common N4 mistake is submitting a crossover or secondary claim with Box 29 or FL54 blank because the biller assumed the primary EOB would attach automatically. It does not. Every COB field must be manually verified and populated from the primary remittance before the secondary claim goes out.
Prevention Checklist — Before Submitting Any Secondary or COB Claim

Run through every item before the secondary claim is submitted:

  • ✅Primary payer's EOB has been received and is for the correct patient, DOS, and service line
  • ✅Primary paid amount entered exactly in Box 29 (CMS-1500) or FL54 (UB-04) — matched cent-for-cent to the primary EOB
  • ✅Primary payer name (Box 11c / FL50) and payer ID (FL51) are correct and recognizable to the secondary payer
  • ✅Primary claim number / document control number entered in Box 11b (CMS-1500) or FL64 (UB-04)
  • ✅Insured information (Box 11a–11d / FL58–FL63) matches the primary insurance card and EOB exactly
  • ✅COB order confirmed — primary is actually primary and secondary is actually secondary per Birthday Rule, MSP rules, or plan COB provision
  • ✅If primary denied the claim — denial EOB is documented and denial reason is communicated correctly on the secondary claim
  • ✅For Medicare MSP situations — MSP status verified via CMS eligibility portal or HIQA before submission
  • ✅Secondary payer's timely filing deadline from primary EOB date confirmed — claim is within the window
  • ✅If secondary payer requires paper EOB attachment — copy of primary EOB is included with the claim
Best Practice — When to Appeal vs. When to Correct & Resubmit

✅ Appeal When ALL of These Are True

  • All COB fields were populated correctly on the original claim — Box 29 or FL54 had the exact primary paid amount, payer name and ID were correct, and insured info matched
  • A copy of the primary EOB was attached where required and the claim data matched it exactly
  • The secondary payer denied it anyway — this indicates a payer-side system or COB data issue and an appeal with the primary EOB documentation should be submitted
  • Always attach the complete primary EOB or remittance advice to the appeal — without it, the appeal will be denied

🔄 Correct & Resubmit First (Most N4 Cases)

  • Most N4 denials are caused by a blank Box 29 or FL54, or by COB fields that were not populated from the primary EOB — correct the fields and resubmit with Resubmission Code 7
  • If the primary has not yet paid or issued an EOB — wait for primary adjudication, then submit the corrected secondary claim with full COB data
  • If the COB payer order was wrong — correct the order, resubmit to the correct primary first, then bill the secondary after receiving their EOB
  • Do not appeal as a first step — fix the root cause data issue and escalate to appeal only after a corrected claim with full COB data is also denied

N227 REMARK CO-16

Incomplete Certificate of Medical Necessity (CMN)

Durable Medical Equipment (DME) · Medical Necessity Documentation · CMS-484 / CMS-849 · DMEPOS
Common CARC Pairing
CO-16 CO-50 DME DMEPOS CMS-1500 CMS-484 CMS-849
What This Means

N227 means the payer received a claim for a service — most commonly a piece of Durable Medical Equipment (DME) or a supply — that requires a Certificate of Medical Necessity (CMN), but the CMN attached to the claim was either missing entirely, was filled out incorrectly, had blank required fields, had conflicting information, was not signed by the ordering physician, or was signed on a date that does not align with the order or delivery date. The payer cannot process the claim until a complete, valid CMN is on file.

ℹ️
What is a Certificate of Medical Necessity (CMN)?
A CMN is a standardized CMS form completed by the ordering physician that confirms a specific piece of DME or a supply is medically necessary for a specific patient. Think of it as the physician's written sworn statement to the payer that says: "I examined this patient, and based on their diagnosis and clinical condition, they need this equipment." Without a valid CMN on file, the DME supplier has no legal or billing basis to receive payment from Medicare or most payers — even if the equipment is genuinely needed by the patient. The CMN is not just paperwork; it is a coverage requirement.
⚠️
N227 Is a Documentation Denial — Not a Coverage Denial
N227 does not mean the equipment or supply is not covered. It means the paperwork supporting medical necessity is incomplete or invalid. Fix the CMN, get it properly signed, and resubmit — in most cases the claim will process. However, if the CMN cannot be corrected retroactively (e.g., the physician refuses to sign or the clinical criteria are not met), the denial becomes permanent.
CMN Forms — Which Form Goes With Which Equipment

Each type of DME that requires a CMN has its own CMS form. Using the wrong form, or using the right form but leaving required sections blank, will trigger N227. Below are the most common CMN forms and what they cover.

CMS Form Equipment / Supply Covered Key Clinical Questions It Asks Most Common Incomplete Field
CMS-484 Home Oxygen Equipment — concentrators, portable units, liquid oxygen Blood oxygen saturation or arterial blood gas test results; flow rate (liters per minute); hours of use per day; estimated length of need Oxygen saturation test result or date of qualifying test missing — this is the single most common N227 trigger for oxygen claims
CMS-849 Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) units Diagnosis causing chronic pain; prior treatment tried and failed; estimated length of need Prior treatment history section left blank — payer needs evidence that other pain management was tried before TENS
CMS-854 Enteral Nutrition (tube feeding) — formulas and pumps Reason patient cannot maintain weight/nutrition orally; type of formula ordered; delivery method (gravity, pump, bolus) Clinical condition section incomplete — physician must specify why oral intake is not possible
CMS-10125 Power Mobility Devices — power wheelchairs and power-operated vehicles (scooters) Mobility limitation diagnosis; face-to-face examination findings; home assessment results; why a cane, walker, or manual wheelchair is insufficient Face-to-face exam documentation or the mobility limitation narrative is missing or vague — CMS requires very specific functional limitation language
CMS-10163 Diabetic Supplies — glucose monitors, test strips, lancets Type of diabetes (Type 1 or Type 2); insulin use; testing frequency ordered by physician Insulin use field blank for Type 2 patients — affects coverage level for test strip quantity
ℹ️
Not All DME Requires a CMN — Know the Difference
Only specific HCPCS codes require a CMN. Many DME items instead require a Detailed Written Order (DWO) — a physician's prescription or order — rather than the formal CMS CMN form. Submitting a DWO when a CMN is required (or vice versa) will trigger N227. Always verify whether your specific HCPCS code requires a CMN, a DWO, or both before submitting the claim.
Where CMN Information Is Reported on the Claim Form
CMS-1500 (DME Supplier / Professional Claims)
  • Box 17 — Referring Provider Name & NPI: The ordering or referring physician who completed and signed the CMN must be identified here — name and NPI must match the physician who signed the CMN exactly
  • Box 17b — NPI of Referring Provider: The ordering physician's NPI must be valid, active, and enrolled as an ordering/referring provider — if the NPI is not in Medicare's ordering/referring database, the CMN is considered invalid regardless of how well it was completed
  • Box 19 / Box 23 — Additional Claim Information: Some payers require the CMN tracking number or order date to be referenced here — check payer-specific billing guidelines
  • Box 21 — Diagnosis Codes: The ICD-10 diagnosis code(s) on the claim must align exactly with the clinical condition documented on the CMN — a mismatch between Box 21 and the CMN diagnosis triggers N227
  • Box 24D — Procedure Code: The HCPCS code billed must be one of the codes covered by the CMN on file — billing a related but different HCPCS code without an updated CMN will cause N227
  • Box 24H — EPSDT / Family Plan: Not commonly used for DME but if populated, must be consistent with the CMN content
CMN Form Itself — Required Sections That Cannot Be Blank
  • Section A — Patient Information: Patient name, Medicare/insurance ID, date of birth, and sex must match the claim exactly — one character off triggers a mismatch edit
  • Section A — Supplier Information: The DME supplier's name and NPI/PTAN must match what the supplier has enrolled with CMS — using an unenrolled location or NPI invalidates the CMN
  • Section A — Place of Service: Where the equipment will be used — typically the patient's home. Must be consistent with the place of service billed on the claim
  • Section B — Clinical Questions: Every question in Section B that has a checkbox or fill-in field must be answered — a single blank answer in Section B is the most common reason a CMN is flagged as incomplete
  • Section C — Narrative / Justification: For certain items (especially power wheelchairs and complex DME), a clinical narrative is required in Section C — a generic or one-sentence response is not sufficient
  • Section D — Physician Signature & Date: The ordering physician must sign and personally date Section D — a stamped signature is not acceptable, a signature date that precedes the face-to-face exam is not valid, and a signature by a non-physician (PA, NP) must follow payer rules on mid-level ordering
✅
Critical rule: The physician signature date on the CMN cannot be earlier than the face-to-face examination date. If the CMN is signed before the exam occurred, it is invalid and will trigger N227 — even if all clinical questions are answered correctly.
Why This Happens — Most Common Root Causes
Missing or Blank CMN Fields
  • Section B clinical questions on the CMN were left partially blank — even one unanswered question in Section B invalidates the entire form
  • The CMN was never obtained before the equipment was delivered — the supplier delivered the item and billed without first securing a completed, signed CMN
  • The CMN was obtained but was never attached to or linked with the claim in the billing system before submission
  • For oxygen claims (CMS-484): the qualifying oxygen saturation test result or arterial blood gas value was not entered on the CMN — this is the most common missing field for oxygen equipment
  • Estimated length of need was left blank — every CMN requires a duration (e.g., 6 months, 12 months, lifetime)
Signature & Dating Errors
  • The physician signed the CMN but did not personally date it — a signature without a date is not valid
  • The CMN was signed before the face-to-face examination took place — the exam must occur first, then the CMN is completed and signed
  • A rubber stamp or electronic auto-signature was used instead of a hand-written or valid electronic signature — many payers and Medicare do not accept stamped signatures on CMNs
  • The CMN was signed by a physician assistant (PA) or nurse practitioner (NP) without verifying whether the payer accepts mid-level ordering providers for that specific HCPCS code
Mismatch Between CMN and Claim Data
  • The diagnosis code on the CMN does not match the ICD-10 code in Box 21 of the claim — both must describe the same clinical condition
  • The HCPCS code billed on the claim is not the code referenced or supported by the CMN — the CMN must specifically justify the item being billed
  • The ordering physician's name or NPI on the CMN does not match Box 17/17b on the claim
  • The patient's name or insurance ID on the CMN does not match what is on the claim — even a nickname vs. full legal name discrepancy can trigger N227
Expired or Outdated CMN
  • The CMN on file has expired — most CMNs are valid for a set period (e.g., 12 months) and recertification is required before the expiration date for ongoing rentals
  • A recertification CMN was not obtained before the billing period covered by the claim — e.g., the original CMN covered months 1–12 but the claim is for month 13 without a recertification
  • The equipment was upgraded or changed but the CMN was never updated to reflect the new item — the CMN must cover the specific HCPCS code being billed
How to Fix It — Step-by-Step
Step 1 — Pull and Review the CMN on File
  1. 1Retrieve the CMN that was on file when the claim was submitted — review every field in Sections A, B, C, and D for completeness
  2. 2Compare the CMN against the claim: confirm the patient name, insurance ID, diagnosis code, HCPCS code, ordering physician name and NPI, and dates all match exactly
  3. 3Check the physician's signature and date in Section D — confirm the signature date is on or after the face-to-face examination date
Step 2 — Identify the Specific Problem
  1. 4If a Section B clinical question is blank — contact the ordering physician's office to complete the missing field. Do not guess or fill it in yourself; only the ordering physician can answer clinical questions on the CMN
  2. 5If the CMN was never obtained — contact the ordering physician immediately to complete and sign a new CMN. Note: some payers allow retroactive completion; others do not. Check the payer's policy before assuming the claim can be salvaged
  3. 6If the CMN has expired — arrange for a recertification CMN to be completed and signed by the physician before resubmitting
  4. 7If there is a data mismatch (diagnosis code, HCPCS, name, NPI) — correct the specific mismatched field on either the CMN or the claim, whichever has the error
Step 3 — Correct the Claim and Resubmit
  1. 8Once the corrected or completed CMN is in hand, submit a corrected claim with Resubmission Code 7 — attach or reference the corrected CMN in your billing system
  2. 9If the payer requires the CMN to be submitted as an attachment, include a clean, legible copy — some payers require the CMN to be faxed or uploaded to their portal rather than mailed
  3. 10Confirm the ordering physician's NPI is active in Medicare's ordering and referring provider database (PECOS) before resubmitting — an unenrolled physician NPI will re-trigger the denial even with a perfect CMN
⚠️
Timely Filing — Do Not Wait on the Physician
While chasing the physician's office for a corrected or missing CMN, watch your timely filing deadline carefully. Submit a timely filing exception request to the payer if needed while the CMN is being corrected — some payers will grant an extension when N227 is the denial reason and the documentation issue is physician-side.
Critical Requirement — Ordering Physician Must Be Enrolled in PECOS

For Medicare DME claims, the ordering physician's NPI must be enrolled in the Medicare Provider Enrollment, Chain and Ownership System (PECOS) as an ordering and referring provider. A CMN signed by a physician who is not enrolled in PECOS is considered invalid by Medicare — even if the form is otherwise perfectly completed. This is one of the most overlooked causes of N227 on DME claims.

Enrollment Check Why It Matters for N227 How to Verify Fix If Not Enrolled
PECOS Enrollment Medicare requires the ordering physician to be actively enrolled in PECOS as an ordering/referring provider — a non-enrolled physician's CMN signature is rejected Search the CMS PECOS lookup tool at pecos.cms.hhs.gov — enter the physician's NPI and confirm ordering/referring status shows as active The physician must submit a PECOS enrollment application — this process takes weeks; the DME supplier cannot bill Medicare until enrollment is confirmed

Mid-Level Ordering Nurse practitioners (NPs) and physician assistants (PAs) can order some DME items but not all — certain HCPCS codes require a physician (MD/DO) signature specifically Check the CMS DMEPOS Policy Article for the specific HCPCS code — it will specify whether a mid-level provider can order that item If the HCPCS code requires an MD/DO signature and the CMN was signed by an NP or PA, the ordering physician must sign a new CMN — the mid-level's signature alone is not sufficient
What NOT to Do
  • Do not fill in blank Section B clinical questions yourself — only the ordering physician can answer clinical questions on the CMN; any alterations by billing staff make the form invalid and create compliance risk
  • Do not alter the physician's signature date, exam date, or any other date on the CMN — falsifying dates on a CMN is a federal compliance violation
  • Do not deliver or bill for equipment before obtaining a signed, completed CMN — the CMN must be in place before the date of delivery for most payers
  • Do not assume the same CMN covers an upgraded or different piece of equipment — if the HCPCS code changes, a new CMN is required
  • Do not appeal N227 without attaching the corrected, completed CMN — an appeal letter alone without the actual documentation will be denied
  • Do not use a CMN completed by a physician who is not enrolled in PECOS for Medicare claims — confirm PECOS enrollment before submitting
🚫
Altering a CMN after it has been signed by a physician — including adding information to blank Section B fields, changing dates, or modifying clinical answers — is considered falsification of a medical record and a federal billing compliance violation. Always go back to the physician for corrections; never make changes yourself.
Prevention Checklist — Before Delivering Equipment and Before Submitting the Claim

Run through every item before delivery and before claim submission:

  • ✅Correct CMN form identified for the specific HCPCS code being billed — right form for the right equipment
  • ✅All Section B clinical questions answered — no blank fields anywhere on the form
  • ✅Physician signature in Section D is a personal, hand-written or valid electronic signature — not a stamp
  • ✅Physician's signature date is on or after the face-to-face examination date — never before
  • ✅Estimated length of need is completed — specific duration entered, not left blank
  • ✅Patient name, insurance ID, and date of birth on CMN match the claim exactly
  • ✅Diagnosis code on CMN matches ICD-10 code in Box 21 of the CMS-1500 exactly
  • ✅HCPCS code covered by this CMN matches the code being billed on the claim
  • ✅Ordering physician's NPI verified as active in NPPES and enrolled in PECOS as ordering/referring provider (Medicare claims)
  • ✅CMN is not expired — if ongoing rental, recertification CMN obtained before the prior CMN's expiration date
  • ✅CMN linked or attached in the billing system before the claim is submitted
Best Practice — When to Appeal vs. When to Correct & Resubmit

✅ Appeal When ALL of These Are True

  • A complete, valid CMN was on file before the claim was submitted — all sections were answered, physician signature and date were present and valid, and no fields were blank
  • The CMN data matches the claim data exactly — diagnosis code, HCPCS code, ordering physician NPI, and patient demographics are all consistent
  • The ordering physician is enrolled in PECOS and their NPI is active in NPPES
  • The payer denied it anyway — this indicates a payer-side documentation matching error and an appeal with the complete CMN attached should be submitted

🔄 Correct & Resubmit First (Most N227 Cases)

  • Most N227 denials are caused by blank Section B fields, a missing physician signature date, or a mismatch between the CMN and claim data — correct the specific issue and resubmit with Resubmission Code 7
  • If the CMN was never obtained — get a completed, signed CMN from the ordering physician and resubmit (check payer's retroactive CMN policy first)
  • If the CMN has expired — obtain a recertification CMN and resubmit the affected claims
  • Do not appeal as a first step when the documentation issue is on your side — correct the root cause first, then escalate to appeal only if a corrected claim with a complete CMN is also denied
